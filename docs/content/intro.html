Introduction

Density Surface Modeling

Density Surface Modeling (DSM) is a powerful and increasingly common method used to estimate the abundance of cetaceans and other mobile marine species from distance sampling survey data. It provides a spatially explicit approach to modeling animal density, addressing limitations of traditional methods that assume uniform density across the study area.

Conceptual Framework

DSM combines two primary components:

  1. Detection Function: Modeling the probability of detecting an animal, which typically decreases with distance from the transect line. This accounts for animals missed during the survey.
  2. Spatial Density Model: Modeling the expected density of animals across the study area as a function of environmental and/or spatial covariates (e.g., bathymetry, sea surface temperature, chlorophyll-a).

The final abundance estimate is derived by integrating the predicted density surface over the entire area.

DSM Outline

The core of DSM relies on the framework of Generalized Additive Models (GAMs) or related regression techniques to link the estimated number of detected animals in a given sampling unit to the effort expended and the predicted detection probability.

1. Estimated Effective Strip Width (\(ESW\))

For a standard line-transect survey, the effective strip width (\(ESW\) or \(\mu\)) is estimated from the fitted detection function, \(\hat{g}(x)\), where \(x\) is the perpendicular distance from the transect: \[ ESW = \int_{0}^{w} g(x) dx \] where \(w\) is the truncation distance, and \(g(x)\) is the estimated probability of detection at distance \(x\). The function \(g(x)\) is often modeled using a key function (e.g., half-normal or hazard-rate) with series expansion adjustments. Standard distance sampling methods must assume that \(g(0) = 1\) because there is no information in detection distances to estimate this value. So, other data must be used to estimate \(g(0)\). So, we treat this quantity as separate from \(ESW\).

2. Spatially Explicit Model for Encounter Rate of Groups

DSM typically employs a two-stage approach. The first stage estimates the detection probability. The second stage uses a regression model to predict the expected number of detected animals in each sampling unit (survey segment).

The model uses the estimated animal density, \(D\), the effective strip width, \(ESW\), the trackline detection probability, \(g(0)\), and the length of the segment, \(L\), to predict the expected number of detected groups (\(E[n]\)) in a segment: \[ E[n] = g(0) \times ESW \times L \times D \] Taking the logarithm (commonly used for count data) yields the linear predictor for the spatial model, \[ \log(E[n_i]) = \log (g(0)_i \cdot ESW_i \cdot L_i) + D_i \] where \(i\) indexed the sample unit (survey segment). This is rearranged for the actual GAM/GLM structure. The term \(\log (g(0)_i \cdot ESW_i \cdot L_i) = \log E_i\) is treated as an offset in the model to account for detection related effort. This yields the spatially explicit model, \[ \log(E[n_i]) = \log E_i + \beta_0 + \sum_{j} f_j(\text{covariates}_{i,j}) \] where \(\beta_0\) is the intercept, and \(f_j(\text{covariates}_{i,j})\) are smooth functions of the spatial and environmental covariates (e.g., longitude, latitude, depth, SST) that together model \(\log(D)\).

4. Spatially Explicit Model for Group Size

In addition to encounter rate of groups, abundance also depends on the expected size of detected groups. Often this is modeled as spatially mean. \(S\). However, environmental conditions might affect group size as well. For example beneficial conditons might lead to large aggregations of individuals increaseing the groups size. So, we generalize the group size to also be a function of environmental conditions through the GLM/GAM model \[ \log(S_k) = \beta_0 + \sum_{j} f_j(\text{covariates}_{k,j}) \] where \(S_k\) is the observed group size for the \(k\)th detected group.

5. Abundance Estimation

The final estimated total abundance (\(\hat{N}\)) for a region, \(\mathcal{R}\), is obtained by integrating the predicted density surface of groups, \(\hat{D}(z)\) times the predicted surface of group size over the entire study area, \[ \hat{N} = A\int_{\mathcal{R}} \hat{D}(z) \hat{S}(z) dz \] where \(\hat{D}(z)\) is the predicted density at a location \(z\) (a function of the environmental and spatial covariates), \(\hat{S}(z)\) is the predicted group size at location \(z\), \(A\) is the total area of . Typically this is executed by calculating abundance over cells of constant covariates, \[ \hat{N} = \sum_{j=1}^M \hat{D}(z_j) \hat{S}(z_j) A_j \] where \(z_j\) is a grid location within \(\mathcal{R}\) and \(A_j\) is the subarea of the region surrounding \(z_j\).

6. Uncertainty Assessment

There are several aspects contributing to uncertainty in the point estimate of abundance, \(\hat{N}\): uncertainty in GLM/GAM parameters and uncertainty in \(ESW\) and \(g(0)\) values. These are treated as constants in the encounter rate model. However, they are estimated from data. Also the covariates are dynamic, so there is variation in covariate values through time. To assess the coefficient of variation (CV) for \(\hat{N}\) we use the variance propogation procedure of Miller et al. (2022)1 which proceeds as

  1. Fit the extended GLM/GAM model: \[ \log(E[n_i]) = \frac{\partial\log E_i(\theta)}{\partial \theta}\delta + \beta_0 + \sum_{j} f_j(\text{covariates}_{i,j}) \] where \(\theta\) are the parameters in the distance sampling and \(g(0)\) analysis and \(\delta\) is a multivariate normal random effect with fixed variance \(V = \text{Var}(\theta)\). This inflates the other GLM/GAM coefficients to account for \(\theta\) (hence \(ESW\) and \(g(0)\)) uncertainty.

  2. Draw \(r\) values of the GLM/GAM coefficients from the posterior distribution using importance sampling. Use the extended model for encounter rate and the initial group size model. This accounts for GLM/GAM parameter uncertainty.

  3. For \(d\) different days of interest predict abundance within the study area using all \(r\) parameter values. Then

\[ \widehat{CV}(\hat{N}) = \frac{\sqrt{Var(\hat{N}_l)}}{Mean(\hat{N}_l)} \] over all \(r\times d\) estimates.

False Killer Whales

False killer whales (Pseudorca crassidens) are a large toothed whale that inhabit sub-tropical and tropical oceanic domains worldwide, and considered rare throughout their range. Three distinct populations of false killer whales are found within the waters of the Hawaiian Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ): two insular populations, one around the main Hawaiian Islands (MHI) and one around the Northwestern Hawaiian Islands (NWHI), and one wide-ranging pelagic population.

Pelagic false killer whales experience a greater degree of overlap with pelagic fishing vessels, and have a history of depredating bait and catch. Bycatch of false killer whales has exceeded allowable levels, leading to the formation of a Take Reduction Team and Take Reduction Plan to reduce serious injury and mortality from fishing operations, as required by the Marine Mammal Protection Act. NOAA Fisheries typically assesses marine mammal stocks at the EEZ level, however, as the EEZ represents a political boundary rather than a biological one, an assessment area beyond the EEZ was developed in an effort for a more comprehensive evaluation of the population. The assessment area for Hawaiʻi pelagic false killer whales is defined by a minimum convex polygon of a 35-km buffer around all genetic, telemetry, sighting, and bycatch location data known or assumed to be of Hawaiʻi pelagic stock animals. Assessing abundance and distribution of pelagic false killer whales throughout their range would improve ongoing efforts to measure and mitigate impacts from fisheries interactions.

Here, we use density surface modeling to obtain abundance estimates for pelagic false killer whales in three regions: the U.S. Hawaiian EEZ, the pelagic stock assessment area, and the broader central Pacific.
